A neurofeedback video game (MindLight) to prevent anxiety in children: A randomized controlled trial

26 May 2016 | Elke A. Schoneveld, Monique Malmberg, Anna Lichtwarck-Aschoff, Geert P. Verheijen, Rutger C.M.E. Engels, Isabela Granic

Study Overview

General Description

Video-games have the potential to provide more effective learning context for children than conventional classroom-based programs for prevention purposes. The aim of the study was to research and evaluate a neurofeedback video game, Mindlight, on the prevention effects of the escalation of anxiety in at-risk children. Specifically, to test the effect of Mindlight on children with elevated levels of anxiety, paying attention to addressing weaknesses in past research designs.

What was done?

136 children who were screened for anxiety were randomly assigned to play either Mindlight or Max and the Magic Marker, as a control game, and would play the games for 5 one-hour sessions, twice a week. This game was chosen as both games follow a child “underdog” avatar who has to conquer fearful obstacles, however Max was not explicitly designed to include evidence-based anxiety-reduction techniques. Self- and parent-reported anxiety was assessed at pre-, post-intervention and 3-month follow up.

Expected that those who played Mindlight would report reduced anxiety at post-intervention and 3-month follow-up as compared to Max In order to consider the game design partially successful, it would induce anxiety but still be an appealing game to play

What were the main results?

At posttest, children in the MindLight group reported lower mean scores on total anxiety symptoms compared to children in the Max group and at follow-up, no differences were found.

Intention-to-treat (ITT) linear regression analyses were performed to test the effect of MindLight compared to Max on anxiety outcomes. No significant effect of game condition on any of the anxiety outcomes were found.

Intent-to-treat analyses revealed an overall significant reduction in child- and parent-reported anxiety, but the magnitude of improvements did not differ between conditions

MindLight was rated significantly more anxiety inducing than Max, suggesting that the game had its intended emotional and exposure effect Consistent with design goals, no differences between the games were found on reported difficulty, relevance, and the extent to which children believed the games would appeal to other children. Children rated Max, compared to MindLight, significantly more appealing to themselves and more likely to induce feelings of flow.

Conclusion

Results showed no differences in decreases in anxiety symptoms between children who played MindLight and those who played Max. Children in both conditions showed significant improvements on anxiety symptoms by the 3-month follow-up, based both on child and parent reports.

Exploratory, puzzle games such as Max and MindLight may train resilience in the face of failure and reappraisal skills, increase self-efficacy and provide short-term distraction (Granic et al., 2014) - all factors related to the reduction of anxiety (e.g., Aldao, 2013; Aldao, Nolen-Hoeksema, & Schweizer, 2010).

Overall this study provided important insights into both games. The study showed that Max seems to have it’s own training mechanics related to improvements in anxiety. Could be used as a purpose-shifted game for anxiety reduction. As well as the study helped to find points of improvement for the design of Mindlight, i.e. more opportunities for “free time” within the game,
