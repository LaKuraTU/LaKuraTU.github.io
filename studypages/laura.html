Treating Childhood Social Anxiety Disorder With Virtual Environments and Serious Games: A Randomized Trial

November 2021 | Deborah C. Beidel, Peter W. Tuerk, Josh Spitalnick, Clint A. Bowers, Krystal Morrison

Study Overview

General Description

Social Effectiveness Therapy for Children (SET-C) is a proven intervention for children with social anxiety disorder (SAD). This form of treatment combines social skills training (SST), structured and therapist-accompanied peer generalization sessions, exposure therapy, and homework assignments. However, some steps in this process are difficult to implement as they require the participation of other peers and/or supervision.
Therefore, in this study, the artificially intelligent, web-based application Pegasys-VR TM was developed to replace the peer generalization sessions and enhance homework compliance. Unlike the traditional peer generalization sessions in Social Effectiveness Therapy for Children (SET-C), where children interact with real people, participants in this study controlled an avatar navigating a VR school setting on an iPad. This environment included various typical locations such as classrooms, schoolyards, and cafeterias, and featured 14 virtual agents with unique personalities. The homework assignments, traditionally carried out in SET-C through direct interactions with other people, were also digitalized and incorporated best practices from serious games. Before the children could freely move around the virtual school and interact with NPCs (non-player characters), they first had to master various serious games, such as recognizing emotions.

What was done?

Over a period of 12 weeks, children diagnosed with Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD) underwent treatment using the 12-week SET-C program. The weekly in vivo exposures and group social skills training (SST) sessions were consistent for all participants. However, for the peer generalization sessions, which followed the group SST, and for the homework assignments, participants were divided into two groups. One group engaged in the traditional real-life peer generalization sessions, while the other group utilized Pegasys-VR TM.
Clinical interviews (ADIS-C/P), parental reports (SPAIC-PV), self-reports (SPAI-C), Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL), Children’s Global Assessment Scale (C-GAS), Clinical Global Impressions Scale (CGI) and behavioral assessments were employed before and after the intervention to assess the impact of the treatment.

What were the main results?

At posttreatment, 63% of the children treated with SET-C and 60% of the children treated with Pegasys-VR TM no longer met diagnostic criteria for social anxiety disorder.
Clinician ratings, parental report and child self-report both showed significally improvement from pre- to posttreatment on all measures. The only group difference was on the SPAIC-PV, indicated superiority for the Pegasys-VR TM group.
There was also no significant group difference for the behavioral assessment test.

Conclusion

Substituting parts of the peer generalization sessions and homework with Pegasys-VR TM features does not negatively affect the effectiveness of SET-C interventions in children with Social Anxiety Disorder (SAD), but it dramatically reduces the organizational time, effort, and the therapist's workload. Therefore, Pegasys-VR could enable more widespread implementation of SET-C, allowing more children to receive effective treatment without an increase in resource allocation.
