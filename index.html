The Effectiveness of Serious Games in Alleviating Anxiety: Systematic Review and Meta-analysis

14. February 2022 | Abd-alrazaq A, Alajlani M, Alhuwail D, Schneider J, Akhu-Zaheya L, Ahmed A, Househ M

One of the nonpharmacological treatments used to reduce levels of anxiety is serious games, which are games that have a purpose other than entertainment. The goal of this review is to summarise previous studies about this topic and conclude the effectiveness of serious games in this context.

Meta-analyse Overview

Focus of the Study
Assess the effectiveness of serious games in alleviating anxiety

Exergames vs. Conventional Exercises: SMD = −0.07

Computerized CBT Games: SMD −0.36

Target Group
Psychologists, Therapists and Health Professionals

Additional Remarks/Results

  • Exergames are as effective as conventional exercises
  • Computerized CBT games are more effective than no intervention
  • Biofeedback games are more effective than conventional video games

Short Study Review

Introduction

The prevalence of anxiety disorders globally is estimated to be about 26,9%. It can affect all age groups and can impair someone’s professional and social life drastically. Anxiety disorders can either be treated pharmacological or non-pharmacological. Even though pharmacological treatments can be effective for anxiety disorders, they often come with a series of disadvantages and can therefore not be used for everyone.

One of those non-pharmacological treatments that is used to reduce anxiety is serious games. These are games that have a purpose other than entertainment. They come in a variety of different types and formats. In this study we will take a look at six different types of serious games. The first format are exergames, which are video games that entail physical exercises (eg, fitness and balance exercises) as part of their gameplay. Second are computerized CBT games, which are games that are based on the principles of Cognitive-Behaviour Therapy (CBT). Next we will have a look at biofeedback games. These are video games, that use electrical sensors attached to the participant to receive information about the participant’s body state and try to influence some of their functions (eg. heart rate). Attention Distraction Games is a format where the player's attention is directed away from another focus or a given event. The fifth type are brain training games, which are games that aim to maintain or improve a users’ cognitive abilities. The last type of games we will have a look at are social skills training games. These try to improve the social skills and mental health of the user.

What is this study about?

The aim of this study is to assess the effectiveness of serious games in reducing anxiety.

The meta-analysis consisted of 22 RCTs. The sample size in the included studies ranged from 30 to 709, with an average of 112.8. The targeted participants were adults (aged >18 years) in 18 studies, adolescents (aged 12-18 years) in 5 studies, children (aged 5-11 years) in 5 studies, and both children and adolescents in 3 studies. Specifically, the mean age of participants reported in 31 studies ranged between 6.6 and 84.2 years, with an average of 34.7 years. The percentage of males reported in 31 studies ranged from 0% to 100%, with an average of 43.2%. The participants’ health conditions varied between studies, and anxiety was the most common (n=7).

There are numerous studies which have tried to shine a light in this field of research. This includes two previous reviews which summarized the results of studies. However these reviews were undermined by certain limitations, for example they focused only on one type of serious games etc. Therefore this study intends to fill this gap in research and give an up-to-date review of the current state of evidence.

What are the results of the study?

The effects in this study were assessed using the standardized mean difference (SMD) because the type of data for the outcome (anxiety level) was continuous, and the instruments used to evaluate were divers.

  • Exergames
    • Exergames vs Conventional Exercises
      • The use of exergames versus conventional exercise was researched in 9 studies. The meta-analysis showed no significant difference in the anxiety levels between the two groups. After a sensitivity analysis two studies were excluded, but the differences stayed not significant.
    • Exergames vs No Intervention
      • In 5 studies exergames were compared with no intervention. A meta-analysis of the results showed no statistically significant difference in the anxiety levels. After a sensitivity analysis, one study was excluded. The degree of heterogeneity decreased significantly from 63% to 28%. Also the differences in anxiety levels changed to be significant, favoring exergames over no intervention.
  • Computerized CBT Games
    • A meta-analysis of the 6 studies comparing computerized CBT games with no intervention, showed a significant difference in the anxiety levels between those groups, favoring computerized CBT games over no intervention. There was a subgroups analysis to look at differences between adults and adolescents. While there was no significant difference in the anxiety levels among adolescents, there was a significant difference among adults (favoring computerized CBT games over no intervention)
  • Biofeedback Games
    • In 5 studies biofeedback games were used as intervention. There was no significant difference between biofeedback games and conventional CBT. However the meta-analysis showed that there is a significant difference in the anxiety levels between biofeedback games and conventional video games, favoring biofeedback games.
  • Others
    • For the other types of study like Attention Distraction Games, Brain Training Games, Social Skills Training Games and two studies which didn’t fit into any of the categories, there was no meta-analysis conducted. Since all of these categories only had 3 or less studies, the authors deemed the samples too small for a meta-analysis. However most of these studies showed no significant effects for any of these games, only attention distraction games showed contradicting results.

Conclusion

This review shows that exergames can be as effective as conventional exercises in alleviating anxiety; computerized cognitive behavioral therapy games and exergames can be more effective than no intervention, and biofeedback games can be more effective than conventional video games. However, our findings remain inconclusive, mainly because there was a high risk of bias in the individual studies included, the quality of meta-analyzed evidence was low, few studies were included in some meta-analyses, patients without anxiety were recruited in most studies, and purpose-shifted serious games were used in most studies. Therefore, serious games should be considered complementary to existing interventions. Researchers should use serious games that are designed specifically to alleviate depression, deliver other therapeutic modalities, and recruit a diverse population of patients with anxiety.

Stakeholders: therapists, patients, app designers (better to develop apps specifically for anxiety)

There is also an urgent need for an inclusive approach when developing these apps and games to include professionals from the gaming industry as well as mental health experts. Technologists and developers are usually very aware of the aforementioned concerns but need medical professionals to avoid falling prey to the temptation of purpose-shifting existing games or designing games for goals that are different from anxiety relief.

Low quality of evidence: The meta-analysis shows that many studies have a low quality of evidence. This is also important information for future studies.
